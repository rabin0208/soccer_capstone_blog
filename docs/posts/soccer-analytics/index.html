Introduction

A national team coach picking a squad for the World Cup faces a common question: does a player who looks great at club level actually play the same way for their country? Scouts face a related one. How do you find pressing forwards in La Liga without writing SQL for every search?

These are the kinds of questions football analysts work on every day. With the World Cup on the calendar, the club-vs-national gap matters even more. A striker who dominates in the league might not fit how a national team actually sets up in a tournament.

For our UBC Master of Data Science capstone with the Trilemma Foundation, we tried to answer these questions by building an automated analytics platform on top of StatsBomb open data.

Each match produces thousands of tracked events like passes, carries, pressures, and shots. The data is there. The harder part is turning roughly 12 million raw events into something scouts, coaches, and analysts can actually use. That was the goal of our project.

A note on the data: our club league analysis focuses on the 2015/2016 season for the top five European leagues (La Liga, Premier League, Bundesliga, Serie A, and Ligue 1), where StatsBomb open data has full seasonal coverage. The wider dataset also includes international tournaments such as the FIFA World Cup, UEFA Euro, Copa América, and AFCON, which we use for the club-vs-national consistency work.

In this post we explain what we built, how the models work, and what we found about player roles and consistency across club and country. You do not need a SQL or machine learning background to follow along.

The Problem

StatsBomb open data is one of the most detailed public football datasets available (StatsBomb 2024). It tracks on-ball actions at sub-second level across 16 competitions in Europe, South America, North America, and international tournaments. That includes shots, passes, carries, pressures, dribbles, and more.

The issue is that the data is hard to work with at scale. Records are nested JSON, competitions are split across many files, and pulling useful player metrics means writing complex SQL. Analysts without coding skills were basically locked out.

Trilemma needed a product that could answer tactical questions without a custom query every time. Things like which players fit a pressing role, or who plays differently for club vs country. We turned that into three research questions:

  1. RQ1 (Player archetypes): What tactical roles exist across European football, and how do they differ by position and competition?
  2. RQ2 (Cross-context consistency): Do players keep similar profiles for club and country, and who specializes in one setting?
  3. RQ3 (Natural language access): Can a non-technical analyst query the dataset in plain English and get accurate answers quickly?

The Data

All data comes from the StatsBomb open dataset through the statsbombpy Python library. We ingested 16 competitions, including the top five European leagues, the FIFA World Cup, UEFA Euro, Copa América, and AFCON. For club football, the main analysis season is 2015/2016, when StatsBomb provides complete match coverage across La Liga, the Premier League, Bundesliga, Serie A, and Ligue 1.

Three tables sit at the core:

Table What it contains Scale
Matches One row per match 3,464 matches
Events One row per on-ball action ~12.2 million rows
Lineups One row per player appearance 165,820 rows

The events table drives most of the analysis. Passes are the most common action (~3.4M), followed by ball receipts (~3.2M), carries (~2.6M), and pressures (~1.1M). There are 88,023 shots with expected goals (xG) values, covering 10,808 players across 308 teams.

A few constraints shaped how we built the models:

  • 270-minute minimum. We only score players with at least 270 minutes in a season, about three full matches. Below that, one good game can skew per-90 stats.
  • Men’s senior competitions only. Open data does not cover everything. La Liga has full seasonal coverage, but some Champions League history is scattered.
  • Data quality. Missing substitution times and positions meant we had to clean the data carefully before modelling.

How the Analytics Work

We focused on how players play, not just goals scored. Two models use the same 13 per-90 StatsBomb features for attack, ball progression, defending, and efficiency:

  1. PCA + K-Means clustering for tactical archetypes
  2. Within-context z-score scoring for club vs national consistency

Both run on the same automated pipeline:

StatsBomb data goes into Google Cloud Storage, then BigQuery. dbt handles the transforms (staging, intermediate, marts). Python scripts add the ML outputs. A Django app serves a Looker Studio dashboard and a Groq chatbot. Dagster runs weekly full refreshes and hourly updates when new files land.

Player Archetype Clustering

We cluster player-seasons after applying the 270-minute minimum (n = 5,469 outfield players, 5,759 including goalkeepers). Goalkeepers are left out of K-Means and get a fixed label because their event profiles look nothing like outfield players.

The 13 per-90 metrics cover the full pitch:

  • Attacking: xG per 90, shots per 90, xG per shot
  • Progression: passes, progressive passes, pass completion %, carries, dribbles
  • Defending: pressures, interceptions, blocks, clearances, duels

We median-impute missing values, clip outliers at the 99th percentile, and standardize features. PCA keeps components that explain 80% of variance. K-Means with k = 5 runs on that reduced space and gives five outfield archetypes.

Choosing k and validating clusters

We did not pick k = 5 by default. The full selection workflow is in Notebook 05 and the exploratory player clustering notebook; production code lives in src/ml/cluster.py.

PCA variance threshold. We retain the smallest number of principal components that explain at least 80% of total variance (typically six with the final 13-feature set). PC1 loads on overall involvement volume; PC2 contrasts defensive actions against attacking output. The scree plot below shows where extra components add less marginal information.

Elbow method (inertia). We tested k = 2–10 on the PCA-reduced space. Inertia (within-cluster sum of squares) drops sharply from k = 2 through k = 4, with diminishing returns after k = 5 — the classic elbow pattern. That suggested five to six tactically distinct groups rather than a coarse high- vs low-activity split.

Silhouette score. Silhouette measures how well each player sits in its assigned cluster versus the nearest alternative (higher is better). It peaked at k = 2 (≈ 0.34), which is common when per-90 profiles form a continuum rather than hard natural boundaries — a two-cluster solution would mostly separate high- vs low-involvement players, not coach-facing roles. Among k = 3–7, k = 5 offered the best trade-off between separation (silhouette ≈ 0.28), interpretable archetype profiles aligned with football analytics practice, and stable cluster sizes. We did not use the Davies–Bouldin index in the final pipeline; inertia, silhouette, and manual profile review drove the choice instead.

Reproducibility and operational stability. Production clustering fixes random_state = 42 and n_init = 10 so assignments are deterministic on the same input data. We did not run bootstrap resampling or adjusted Rand indices in the shipped pipeline; when the underlying player pool grows, archetype labels in seeds/cluster_labels.csv are reviewed manually to stay interpretable. Unit tests in tests/test_cluster.py guard preprocessing (median imputation, 99th-percentile clipping, standardization) and output shape.

External validity. Chi-square tests on position × archetype cross-tabs (Notebook 07) provide a downstream check: center backs concentrate in Defensive Anchor and Low Activity, while forwards skew toward Pressing Forward and Creative Winger. The clusters align with football intuition rather than arbitrary splits.

Archetypes describe how a player is used in a given season, not a fixed career label. Lionel Messi is mostly Creative Winger, but shows up as Pressing Forward in Copa América 2024. That fits Argentina’s more compact setup in that tournament.

Club vs National Consistency Scoring

We looked at 489 dual-context players with at least 270 minutes in both club and national football. Each of the 13 features is standardized within context. Club z-scores compare a player to other club players only. National z-scores compare to other national-team players only.

From there we compute:

  • Performance scores: how strong a player is vs peers in each setting
  • Consistency score: how similar a player’s profile is across contexts (1 minus the mean absolute z-gap)
  • Four performance quadrants: based on median splits on club and national performance

Progressive passing into the attacking third was the biggest gap between club-focused and national-focused specialists. An Elite player beats cohort medians in both settings. But a stable profile is not the same as a strong one. Many players look similar across contexts and still rank below average in both.

Validating consistency scores

The consistency model is simpler than clustering, but we still checked it end to end:

  • Within-context standardization ensures club z-scores compare only to other club players (and national z-scores to national-team peers), so league or tournament level differences do not dominate the gap metric.
  • Eligibility filter: only players with ≥ 270 minutes in both contexts enter the cohort (n = 489), matching the clustering minimum and stabilizing per-90 rates.
  • Formula checks: the weighted performance score and consistency formula (1 − mean|z_gap|) are unit-tested in tests/test_consistency.py and walked through in Notebook 03.
  • Pipeline alignment: end-to-end validation confirmed matching row counts across dbt marts and ML outputs (5,759 clustered player-seasons; 489 dual-context players scored). See the consistency methodology appendix and docs/consistency.md for full definitions.
  • Face validity: outputs were sanity-checked against known dual-context players (e.g., Serge Gnabry as International Specialist in the dashboard explorer).

What We Found

RQ1: Position Drives Role More Than League

Chi-square tests showed that position drives archetype assignment more than competition. A few patterns stood out:

  • Center backs mostly land in Defensive Anchor and Low Activity. Attacking clusters are rare.
  • Forwards and attacking mids lean toward Pressing Forward and Creative Winger.
  • Central mids spread across Creative Playmaker, Pressing Forward, and Low Activity. That reflects overlapping box-to-box roles.
  • Full backs often end up as Creative Winger or Creative Playmaker when their profiles lean on carries and progressive passing.

League effects matter too, but less than position. Serie A has the largest Low Activity share among the top five leagues. Bundesliga and La Liga have relatively more Pressing Forwards. The same position can shift 5–15 percentage points in archetype share between leagues, so it helps to filter by position before comparing competitions.

RQ2: A Third of Players Specialize in One Context

This question is especially relevant around a World Cup. Coaches often have to pick players who look strong in the league but may not match the national team’s system. Our consistency scores are meant to help with exactly that kind of decision.

Of 489 dual-context players:

About two thirds of the cohort rank above or below the peer median in both settings, not just one. When we compared Club Specialist and International Specialist players, the biggest gaps were in passes into the attacking third per 90, interceptions per 90, and carries into the final third per 90. System and role differences explain the split more than goal output.

Defenders had slightly higher mean consistency than forwards. Attacking roles seem to shift more between club tactics and national setups.

RQ3: Ask Questions in Plain English

The Groq-powered Django chatbot answers plain-English questions from BigQuery in under ten seconds. We tested it on typical analyst questions like player rankings by archetype, cross-context comparisons, and competition filters. It applied the same rules as the dashboard, including the 270-minute threshold.

Advanced Mode shows the generated SQL. A read-only guard blocks destructive queries.

The Product

We shipped a Django web app with two interfaces:

  • Looker Studio dashboard: pre-computed results (archetype distributions, consistency quadrants, player rankings) with filters for position, competition, and archetype. Useful for regular reporting and sharing with non-technical staff.
  • Plain-English chatbot: handles ad-hoc questions that fixed dashboard charts do not cover, with SQL shown for audit.

You can try the live app yourself at http://34.11.235.252/. It includes the dashboard and the chatbot, with suggested questions like “What cluster does Messi belong to?” or “Who are the Elite players in La Liga?” For a walkthrough of how it works, watch our demo video.

We built both because analysts need broad visual exploration and specific one-off queries. Notebooks alone were not practical for non-technical users. A dashboard alone could not replace flexible querying without Looker edit access.

Strengths: Dagster refreshes all data weekly with no manual steps. The full product runs from a single docker compose up and is documented for handover.

Limitations: the chatbot only handles single-step queries. Multi-step questions (like tracking a player’s archetype across seasons before comparing national peers) are not supported yet. Both interfaces cover men’s competitions only. Dashboard charts cannot be changed without Looker Studio access.

Why This Matters

Archetypes describe playing style, not transfer value or injury risk. Consistency scores help with national team context, not squad chemistry. Still, the outputs are useful in practice.

For Scouts and Analysts

  • Archetype labels help profile scouting targets and check squad balance before a transfer window.
  • Consistency scores support national team selection by showing who performs in both contexts and who specializes.
  • The chatbot lets analysts query the marts without writing SQL for every question.

For Fans

The same ideas show up when you watch games:

  • A player can look great at club level and different internationally, or the other way around.
  • Tactical systems shape profiles. Messi as Pressing Forward at Copa América 2024 says something about how Argentina set up in that tournament.
  • Terms like “pressing forward” or “progressive passer” have a concrete meaning behind them in the data.

Conclusion

Trilemma analysts had no scalable way to pull tactical insights from StatsBomb event data without complex SQL. We built three research outputs on one automated pipeline to address that.

For player archetypes, we found five tactical roles across 5,759 player-seasons in 16 competitions. Position mattered more than league when assigning those labels. For club vs national consistency, about 36% of dual-context players specialized in one setting, while 32% performed above the peer median in both. For natural language access, a Groq LLM could turn plain English questions into accurate BigQuery SQL.

We also learned that the product shape matters as much as the models. A dashboard and a chatbot cover different needs, and neither works as well on its own.

Key Takeaways

  • Five outfield archetypes (plus goalkeepers) come from 13 per-90 StatsBomb features using PCA + K-Means
  • Position drives role assignment more than league or competition
  • 36% of dual-context players specialize in club or national football. System differences, not goal output, drive most of the split
  • A plain-English chatbot lets non-technical analysts query 12+ million events without SQL
  • The platform refreshes weekly and deploys with one Docker command

Quick Reference: Key Terms

Archetype: A tactical role label (e.g., Pressing Forward, Creative Playmaker) based on a player’s per-90 event profile in a given season.

Per-90 metrics: Stats scaled to a full 90-minute game so players with different minutes can be compared.

Consistency score: How similar a player’s tactical profile is for club vs country (1 = identical, 0 = very different).

xG (Expected Goals): An estimate of shot quality based on distance, angle, and defensive pressure.

Quadrant: One of four groups (Elite, Club Specialist, International Specialist, Underperformer) based on performance vs peers in each context.
